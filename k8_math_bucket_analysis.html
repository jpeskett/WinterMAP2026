Key Findings

  • 58% of non-growing students PASSED our internal Alpha tests at 90%+ — this is the single most alarming signal. It means our tests give false confidence about student readiness.
  • 45 students (23%) never even took a mastery exam — they are stuck in the pipeline, enrolled in courses but never reaching the assessment gate.
  • 37 students (19%) tried the test but couldn't pass — the curriculum isn't teaching what the test measures, or students are being tested at the wrong level.
  • Alpha School Austin dominates Bucket 2 with 25 of 45 students — a campus-specific efficiency problem.
  • Average growth across all 196 students is ~1.05x — essentially flat, barely above typical growth, and far below the 2x target.
  • Deep dive students (Lydia Cobas, George Troxell, Elle Zak, Lucius Walker) are all in Bucket 3, confirming the systemic pattern.

Bucket 1: Curriculum Not Effective (37 students)

These students took the Effective Grade Level test but FAILED to pass at 90%. They put in effort — they attempted the assessment — but the curriculum did not prepare them to succeed. This points to a mismatch between what is being taught and what is being tested.

Key Patterns

  • Average growth: 0.95x — essentially flat. 13 students were between 1.5x-2.0x, meaning they were growing but still missed the 90% pass mark.
  • 41% are in Zearn courses, 35% in Math Academy — the curriculum source doesn't seem to be the differentiator; the gap is in depth of mastery.
  • Several students like Elijah Knecht (89.3%), Sofia Page (88.2%), and Ozzie Lowe (86.1%) are within striking distance of 90% — targeted intervention could push them over.
  • Others like Eastwood Washburn (16%), Vivian Ajouz (20%), and Brooke Tracy (43.8%) have fundamental gaps — they may be placed at the wrong level entirely.

Bucket 2: Curriculum Not Efficient (45 students)

These students didn't finish their course or never took the mastery exam. They are stuck in the pipeline — enrolled but never reaching the assessment point. This is a system efficiency failure: time is being spent but the pathway isn't producing measurable outcomes.

Key Patterns

  • Alpha School Austin accounts for 56% (25/45) of this bucket — a campus-specific pacing problem.
  • All 45 students have ZERO test attempts in the Alpha test system — they never reached the assessment gate.
  • 60% are in Math Academy courses, but many are in supplementary/hole-filling tracks (Edia G4 Supp, Edia G6 Supp) that don't have a clear test gateway.
  • Several students show high growth (Anaya Lenington 1.86x, Arman Caberwal 1.83x, C.J. Wright 1.80x) but still didn't take the test — suggesting the pipeline, not the student, is the bottleneck.
  • Outliers like Cade Bustamante (-75x) and Tanner Klentz (-50x) have extreme negative growth values suggesting data anomalies or withdrawal.

Bucket 3: Tests Not Rigorous Enough (114 students)

These students finished their grade-level course, passed the Alpha test at 90%+, but STILL didn't grow 2x on MAP. This is the largest bucket (58%) and the most alarming: our internal assessments are certifying mastery that external assessments don't confirm. The Alpha tests may be measuring procedural recall rather than the conceptual depth MAP requires.

Key Patterns

  • 87 students have confirmed 90%+ Alpha test passes; 27 mastered EG via course completion without Alpha test data in system.
  • Growth multiples range from -1.5x to 1.91x — many are close to 2x but the gap represents real learning that isn't happening.
  • Deep dive students Lydia Cobas, George Troxell, Elle Zak, and Lucius Walker are all in this bucket — confirming the systemic pattern.
  • Many passed lower-grade Alpha tests (e.g., passing G2 while in G5) — demonstrating mastery of below-grade content MAP doesn't test.
  • Some scored 96-100% on Alpha but grew only 0.5x or less — a massive validity gap.

Three Systemic Signals

Signal 1: Assessment Validity Crisis

58% of non-growing students PASSED our tests. Internal mastery certification is not translating to external measurement. Alpha tests may measure procedural recall rather than conceptual depth.

Signal 2: Pipeline Efficiency Failure

45 students never took a test. They are stuck in the system — enrolled but never reaching assessment. Completion rates, pacing, or course assignment are breaking down.

Signal 3: Curriculum-Assessment Misalignment

37 students tried and couldn't pass. The curriculum isn't teaching what tests measure, or students are assessed at the wrong level. Many have 3+ attempts — effort without progress.

Recommendations by Bucket

Bucket 1: Curriculum Not Effective

  • Audit the gap between curriculum content and test content — if students score 70-89%, targeted gap-filling may close the difference.
  • Review assessment level placement — are students being tested on content they haven't been taught?
  • Implement pre-test diagnostic to identify specific skill gaps before course assignment.

Bucket 2: Curriculum Not Efficient

  • Establish a time-to-test SLA — no student should be in a course for more than a semester without taking the mastery exam.
  • Flag supplementary-only enrollments — students in hole-filling courses without a core pathway are invisible to the mastery system.
  • Create automated alerts when students pass completion thresholds but haven't been assigned a test.

Bucket 3: Tests Not Rigorous Enough

  • Redesign Alpha tests to include MAP-aligned items — add multi-step reasoning, non-routine problems, and application questions that mirror MAP's cognitive demand.
  • Add a MAP readiness diagnostic layer after Alpha mastery — students who pass Alpha should face a second-tier assessment before being certified grade-level ready.
  • Accelerate curriculum placement — if a student masters G3, immediately move them to G4 rather than letting them plateau.
  • Address the structural grade-level mismatch — passing a below-grade test does not help grow on a grade-level MAP assessment.

Bottom Line for Leadership

58% of students who failed to hit 2x MAP growth actually PASSED our internal Alpha tests — meaning our assessments are giving false confidence. The #1 systemic fix is not more curriculum hours; it is rebuilding our internal tests to match the cognitive rigor of MAP, closing the gap between what we certify and what students can actually demonstrate on an external, grade-level assessment.
